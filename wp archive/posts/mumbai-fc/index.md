An Ode to the Yellow Brigade

_“Fortune's always hiding_,

_I've looked everywhere_,

_I'm forever blowing bubbles_,

_Pretty bubbles in the air._”

 

As someone who has lived in South Bombay all my life, the Cooperage was nothing more than a landmark or an astro-turf pitch where my South Bombay friends paid exorbitant prices to have a kickabout. Little did I know how much the perpetually half-empty 5000 stadium could come to mean me in a matter of days.

A few days into the new year, a friend of mine (who plays for the U17 Mumbai FC team) offered a few of us tickets to what was dubbed as the ‘Maharashtra Derby’. Unbeknownst at the time, that Saturday evening was to have a profuse effect on the lives of the few of us who took our friend up on the tickets. With little knowledge of the I-League and even lesser knowledge about ‘our’ team, Mumbai FC, we sat ourselves in the ‘VIP section’ of Cooperage, a sparsely populated stand that had the occasional expletive thrown at the opposition. Underwhelmed by the experience up until half-time, on our way to the chai-wallah outside, we chanced upon the Yellow Brigade for the first time.

The scene was unreal.

The following paragraph is bound to be rife with cliché, and for that the author apologizes.

A composite of yellows of a single hue yet faces so colorful, whose initial glances at boys who looked ever so out of place were not ones of judgment but ones of warmth. That block of the stadium, Block F I believe, cannot, by any stretch of imagination, be called a ‘Sea of Yellow’. A more appropriate comparison would be to a waterfall – compact, raucous, unrelenting and tenacious. We abandoned the relative comforts of our seats, not for any reason but the fact that the legion of fans were oblivious to the existence of those seats in the first place – on their feet, no questions asked. As the players reassembled on the hallowed Cooperage turf was when the ‘Yellow Brigade’ showed their true colors. That entire half had spectacular running commentary, courtesy the songs, dances and extremely innovative chants of 35-odd individuals with no agenda but the benefit of the team on their mind. The chants were ingenious – both provocative and reactive, they pushed Mumbai FC when they needed it the most and, much to my amusement, never failed to put the opponent in their place.

Most chants were easy to pick up on – the “Let’s go Mumbai” and the “Chutiya Referee” were intuitive. However, these were a gateway to some of the most incredible taunts I have come upon – “Misal pav/ neem dosa/ momo pe bhari kaun? Vada Pav Vada Pav!” or the classic “Who are ya?” were crooned liberally, every time the oppositions support even murmured. It was clear that at the Cooperage, there was only one set of fans that ran the show. Our oblivion to player names, their history and our complete lack of context was, admittedly, quite shameful and a tad embarrassing, however after that game every player was religiously stalked, ensuring that we came to the next game well updated with all the inside jokes. After the win that evening, the Yellow Brigade waited patiently till the players came over to laud the supporters to rapturous applause – after which they wrapped the giant flexes, banners and flags.

What began as a an innocuous Saturday evening plan on the invitation of a college friend soon became an inextricable part of my life, one that defines Bombay, and all its resilience.

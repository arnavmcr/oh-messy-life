An Hour with ‘The Getaway’

 

2:08pm || 20/6/16

With a chaotic, draining weekend now behind me, I finally had the opportunity to spend some time with the new Red Hot Chili Peppers album, ‘The Getaway’. I have always maintained that RHCP, rather inexplicably, are perennially on any ‘rainy day’ playlist I make, and the backdrop of the first undulating showers set the mood for this afternoon perfectly.

<!--more-->

2:15pm

A song and a half in, the odes to California have already begun. ‘The Getaway’ is a depiction of great restraint on the band’s part, with mature electronic sensibilities and a (comparatively) plain bass-line replacing the usual chaos. The presence of female backing vocals poses more questions than it answers a rather unnecessary addition to the otherwise dark track. Lead single, ‘Dark Necessities’ juxtaposes slap bass and piano chord progressions with the popular electronic clap. Klinghoffer features but sporadically, using ridiculously processed tones to add subtle depth to the track.

2:26pm

While the band is no stranger to writing the powerful, anthemic ballad, it almost feels like sections of ‘softer’ songs have been interchanged with the funk goodness we all love. This sonic construction is evident on ‘We Are Red’, and is off-putting at best. Once again, the listener sits confused with the raw ferocity with which the verse has been written which transitions into the mellow chorus. This trend of anti-climactic songs is worrying, but the choruses are a real delight.

The best song of the album thus far, ‘The Longest Wave’ reminisces of the frivolous yet comforting side of the band we know and love. Warm, deliberate chord-progressions and the standard bass debauchery make this track a well-arranged, gratifying addition to the album.

2:29pm

‘Goodbye Angels’ follows the trend of a literal amalgamation of funk and pop. Its verses sound dichotomous with the massive bass-line being contradicted by the guitar sections. However, the instrumental section toward the end of the song uses all frequencies impeccably.

2:38pm

Transporting the listener to the beaches of Jamaica where the reggae movement began, ‘Sick Love’ coalesces both reggae and funk into four minutes of easy-listening, sing-alongable ‘happy music’. The strumming pattern coupled with the breakbeat drums give Kiedis’s the perfect opportunity to continue his ode to California. The piano makes its second appearance, and on these occasions provides the band with the deftness of touches.

Moderately hasty, ‘Go Robot’ and its spacey electronics comprise a track doused in large-amounts of minimalism. A refined, intentional track which explores the electronic side the band has shunned, ‘Go Robot’ is a departure from the hard-hitting, straight-up music we associate with RHCP.

‘Feasting on the Flowers’ follows a similar structure to its preceding track. Both lack the cutting edge yet have enough credibility to make their way on to the record. They do, however, languish in the doldrums of the album. Mechanically written and arranged, they display nothing significant other than the use of legitimate electronics.

 

2:43pm

Three minute food break, note to self – do not try eating an entire roti in two bites.

2:46pm

‘Detroit’ presents itself as an industrial sounding, brutish testament to the city. Its hard-hitting opening riff and its pounding chorus makes it a rock ‘n’ roll delight. The chorus consoles and yearns simultaneously, with squealing instruments layered beneath.

2:52pm

‘This Ticonderoga’, admittedly required some Googleing on my part, continues to baffle me with its title, but the music is back to its hard-hitting best. Running bass-lines, fret-board caressing guitar riffs being topped off with the beloved, nonsensical lyrics comprise the only song that manages the transition from funk to ballad seamlessly.

The latest song to be dubbed as the best on the album, ‘Encore’ is stellar rainy day music. It’s sober, powerful arrangement and the use of a massive string section elevates both the song and album. Smith’s use of the hi-hat and Klinghoffer’s timely, transcendental riffs stand out.

3:00pm

Crossing the line from sober to too sober, ‘The Hunter’ is lazy version of the song it succeeds. It sounds lackadaisical; the lyrics are neither profound nor nonsensical. Instrumentally too, the song falls short of the high mark the band sets for themselves.

3:07pm

Capping of the album, ‘Dreams of a Samurai’ opens with an extensive piano solo which fades into a looped bass riff. Guitar scratches and a mature drum beat follow the vocals into a cacophonic chorus. The piano reveals itself once again, immersing itself into an-almost directionless song. Klinghoffer shows off his prowess while the Flea continues playing the same bass-line through the song. The song descends into an instrumental section which has a sense of disorganization that exceeds standard RHCP levels.

_Overall, ‘The Getaway’ lacks in cohesion. The album sways from electronics to the scat we enjoy, but has very little in the middle. The average track on the album is definitely sub-par, and the ability to incorporate these levels of textures will not be an easy ask.  It does however; make incredible additions to the band’s repertoire on the lighter side. The band’s status as genreless heavyweights and legends in the truest sense of the word is more than secure and will be sempiternally. This album is not a step forward neither is it a step behind, it is simply a timely reminder that the band is capable of writing songs that festivals and arenas will sing along to, no questions asked._

November 2014 was a watershed period in my life. I immersed myself into college life wholly. I attended my first two music festivals, and hosted two students at my home as part of various exchange programmes. Both of them were about my age, but were from halfway across the world - Belgium and Italy.

An extraordinarily chaotic month that was, with broken English and gestures playing an essential role.

Fast forward to May 2015, and I was sitting on a plane to Milan to stay with Matteo, a dashing, fun-loving nineteen year-old. We had been dubbed as twins, with remarkable likenesses. Our demeanour and attitudes were suspiciously identical, which made both his stay in Bombay and mine in Sarezzo an effortless process.

<!--more-->

The Mariannini residence was located on a hillock in Sarezzo, a suburb of a suburb of Milan. Blessed with views of the magnificent Italian Alps on one side and picture-perfect Italian villages on the other, the week I spent was surreal. From listening to Muse in a tiny car, weaving through the cobbled streets to eating pizza at every meal, memories of Italy fill me with this inexplicable nostalgia and this uncontrollable happiness. Every moment of that trip has given me stories to last a lifetime, be it (accidentally) drinking alcohol for the first time or watching a match at the mighty San Siro.

Away from all the fanfare and the typical tourist garb, what made the trip surreal was the people - Matteo and his family accepted me into their home like one of their own. Although language was a barrier, their perennial smiles said it all. One evening, the entire family gathered at their home for a dinner. Not unlike Indian families, this gathering had upwards of twenty people - aunts, uncles, cousins, dogs and the head of the family - Grandma Mariannini came together to truly induct me into their amazing traditions. Never have I been hugged as tight as I was by Matteo's Grandmother. 'Maia' as Matteo was known as, is one of the most loving people I know, be it with his friends or family. Walking the streets of Sarezzo with him was like walking with a celebrity, every person who walked by was hugged or kissed by him, and I was inevitably introduced. He was an Italian through and through, with his rugged beard and excessive drinking habits.

> Italy may not have the best economy, but it has the best families

This statement Matteo made (in his typically broken English), during one of our evidently philosophical conversation, epitomised the week. It turns out, that no matter where you may be on this planet, that families are irreplaceable.

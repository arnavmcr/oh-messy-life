# April ‘25

### What is this and why am I doing this?

Hello. This is a monthly newsletter update thingi that I’d like to do to stay more connected with my friends. It is also my attempt at vulnerability, which I’m working on. If you are reading this, you are important to me and our friendship is something I value. Thank you for taking the time out.

This will also not be good writing, but it will be simple and unnuanced.

Also - feel free to reply/not reply at your own pace/convenience. The goal with this is not to force conversation or engage. It is to asynchronously, honestly and (within reasonable bounds) meaningfully remain “in touch” or connected with people I care about.

### Shark Tank India

I spent a lot of this month winding down my day with an episode or two of Shark Tank India. To see the jugadu-ness, ambition and grit of other entrepreneurs was tremendously inspiring. It was also reassuring to see how most consumer-facing businesses have managed to scale, of course through good products and clever marketing etc, but riding the secular wave of urban Indian upper middle class' discretionary spending. That is good, this is a space that I am very bullish on.

What inadvertently happened through watching Shark Tank was that I got weirdly red-pilled into a glamourised and dramatised view of entrepreneurship. The extreme, suicidal and all-or-nothing view of starting up. Perhaps parts of these are both true and necessary (I still think a lot of it is applicable), but emulating these paths has made Putt One seem more like work and less like play, which was the original intention. It has also taken an unforeseeable toll on the rest of my life (relationships, friends, family, other projects etc etc), something I underestimated and still find difficult to accept.

I guess all of this is in line with my self proclaimed theme for this year, as laid out in January, of "focus", but April saw me stressing more about focusing than actually focusing, an isolating inner monologue that seems unshakeable and is very consuming.

### Temptation Island

In this inopportune, vulnerable time where the pressure of focusing skirted with desperation, there came a deliverance that I declined. In parts, this was a "get out of jail for free" card, in other parts it was a cop-out. Both are true. Ultimately, I considered this opportunity more a cop-out from the path that I have chosen and that it was a disservice to the direction I am trying to go in.

Another feature of making this decision was that I felt it to be largely driven by exaggerated mathematics of how this would be perceived, overly solving for optics and positioning, and hypotheticals of "how this would look." The aforementioned inner dialogue exacerbated this, making this an overly convoluted and tedious decision, sometimes divorced from reality. Thanks to everyone who helped ground me and reminded me of things about myself that I forgot.

### Kaun banega crorepati

All my professional life has been built on well-thought out decisions and asymmetric upside. A big feature of this has been some kind of frugality when it comes to major cash outlay. As Putt One reaches a juncture where such an outlay is necessary to actually build scale, it is frightening. Opportunity cost is easier to rationalise, because the equation is imaginary. It is a perceived loss in value. But actual CAPEX (to rent a space or to invest in moulds or whatever) is a very real transaction.

It is uncomfortable because it's unprecedented for me, and also not something I've seen a lot around me (except for my business school homies who have taken on some CAPEX in a slightly different sense), so building comfort is not straightforward. If anyone has a litmus test or mental model or just some way of looking at making a large CAPEX, please let me know :)

### Other random things

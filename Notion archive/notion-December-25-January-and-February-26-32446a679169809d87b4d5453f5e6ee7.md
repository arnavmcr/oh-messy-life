# December ‘25, January and February ‘26

### What is this and why am I doing this?

Hello. This is a monthly newsletter update thingi that I’d like to do to stay more connected with my friends. It is also my attempt at vulnerability, which I’m working on. If you are reading this, you are important to me and our friendship is something I value. Thank you for taking the time out.

This will also not be good writing, but it will be simple and unnuanced.

Also - feel free to reply/not reply at your own pace/convenience. The goal with this is not to force conversation or engage. It is to asynchronously, honestly and (within reasonable bounds) meaningfully remain “in touch” or connected with people I care about.

#### The hubris of knowing

For my zillenial cohort, 2016 was the last year where we didn't know anything. As we stepped into adulthood, no one feigned knowledge. Life shaping decisions of degrees and colleges were hypothesis-led, nervous choices vaguely informed by logic. There was much weight attached to these decisions but little regard, the general consensus seemed to be that things will be figured out. Our lack of knowledge was immaterial, and all that mattered was our collective ability to make the most of what was in front of us.

In not knowing grew humility, a submission to youth. I/we spent this period collectively squeezing the life out of each day, unclear but united. There was little judgement and little care -- just a universal commitment to be able to pour ourselves into the world around us.

In the decade since, we began to know things. It started with the most accurate simulation of the professional world - college fests (I am being facetious pls don't @ me), and followed us into the actual professional or post-graduate world. Suddenly, we began to know the world beyond us, tempted by its promises and grand narratives. We started to know that the world is not round, but is flat and straight, inviting us to run on, collecting promotions and degrees and paycheques as we went along. In knowing we looked ahead and behind, rarely sideways as we once did.

Eventually, we found ourselves in the hubris of knowing. Armed with worldviews we had been fed and that we had been taught to eloquently defend, unable or unwilling to look beyond what we knew. After a decade of building these walls of hubris, 2025 felt like a collective dissolution of all of it.

While it's hard to pinpoint a single moment that broke the camel's back, I hypothesise this dissolution as a response to increased ambiguity. As the world around us feels more irreversible (the MBA/marriage epidemic) and our roles within it begin to feel daunting and unclear, we've begun to crave the simplicity of not knowing. This is not nostalgia, nor is it escapism, it is a reprioritisation.

As this year begins (yes yes I am aware it is March, consider this between the Gregorian calendar and the Financial calendar), I do not know much. I am glad that most people around me also don't know much (if unclear -- this is a compliment). I'm very grateful for this collective stoner indifference, a laissez faire approach to happenings, sans judgement and sans hubris.

#### A decade of wonder

Of the few things I do know, top of the list is that a December music festival is essential to my being. This unintentional ritual serves a few fundamental purposes: a reminder of wonder, an opportunity to reflect on the year passed, a free pass to do drugs, and a celebration of friendship.

This December (and January) was no different: Wonderfruit a window into what might be, and Lollapalooza into how far we've come. I wrote about watching Green Day at Lollapalooza ( [here](https://tidal-seahorse-bc0.notion.site/March-25-1c746a6791698057a091fbc8cce53d3e?source=copy_link) and [here](https://www.aftereod.com/archives/welcome-to-paradise-delayed-arrival-millenial-dream-green-day)) last year, watching Linkin Park was near-identical in its memorableness, with sweat, some blood and many tears.

Watching these two bands, in consecutive years, in 'my' city, would be unfathomable a decade ago, when this music festival ritual began. There is much to be proud of, as a scene and as its participants over these ten years. Yet, Linkin Park's set felt like the closing of a chapter. The hipster idealism and the punk bias-for-action that formed the basis of local music scenes, at least in the first half of the aforementioned 2016-2026 decade, is now well and truly a fantasy.

While mourning this worldview's slow descent into irrelevance, it is in part due to the aforementioned zillenial cohort outgrowing a scene that has grown, but not in tandem as its participants. This imbalanced growth makes reckoning with this 'closing chapter' easier to swallow -- the scene is not inherently better or worse than it was, maybe it's us who have changed (alternatively: the night's forever young, it's us that get old).

If Lollapalooza exemplified having come of age, Wonderfruit exemplified tip-toeing on the precipice of possibility. Being anonymous and untethered in a mystical faraway land shocked me into realising that the world is much, much, much larger than I had thought. While this was in part the sheer absurdity of the festival (at times looking like a zombie apocalypse with bodies lying k-holed on the floor, or the layout that felt like an endless, recursive maze), there were two other reasons for this realisation: its proximity and its monumentality.

Quite literally, Wonderfruit was proximal (flying to Thailand at times can be cheaper than flying to Goa) and seamless (thank you Aditi, Shivani, and Ganesh for letting me join your extremely well organised trip!! it has set an unbelievably high bar for planned and unplanned masti). The closeness and ease of attending Wonderfruit could be said of most vacations. Given my upcoming temporary but protracted South East Asia stay, this trip had an added dimension of tangibility -- living in South East Asia is now an addition to the universal set of possibilities. The possibility of, well, possibility made every part of the trip an unintentional immersive evaluation, one that was passed with flying colours. The physical proximity but more so the feeling of possibility that came from this proximity reminded me that the world is large, and there is much that I am yet to see.

![](https://tidal-seahorse-bc0.notion.site/image/attachment%3Afa009670-e4d2-49e9-b861-44d9c0b98168%3AScreenshot_2026-03-15_at_7.23.16_PM.png?table=block&id=32446a67-9169-80d1-9308-ca8d7ba75517&spaceId=743a16a0-8cd9-4648-bd76-d789f79e3cf6&width=1160&userId=&cache=v2)

The monumentality of Wonderfruit was not as loud or in-your-face as watching Linkin Park (infinitely more grand). It was in watching múm, a band I didn't know still performed, sat on the grass under the stars, with the warm glimmer of friendship and serotonin slowly enveloping my veins (múm are the wacky cousins of Sigur Ros. Both are Icelandic, experimental, cinematic, ethereal etc etc).

Watching múm (and Wonderfruit generally) reinforced life design choices: I want to be able to just do things. At the end of a year (2025) which ended poles apart from how it started, reminding myself of the joys of being able to live unconstrained was monumental. Much of my ability to 'do things' came from doing the things others would tell me for 5 days a week, and doing my own thing(s) on weekends and during my 21 designated leave days.

I was previously repulsed by this arithmetic, as visible in my blood reports and dark circles in 2023-24. Ironically, my blood report makes for significantly more bleak reading after I tried to escape 'living for the weekend'. So that leaves me unclear on how to design a life where I can continue to 'just do things', bank account, cholesterol and sanity preserved.

Yet, fleeting moments (like watching múm) have now become pinnacles, exemplars that will guide decisions. That these decisions feel both tangible and within touching distance added much wonder to a 'fruit' filled weekend ;)

#### Left write left

My idealistic plan of filling the many months until I go with several two-week long projects has surprisingly played out, albeit imperfectly.

One of these two-week sprints was writing an [After EOD piece analysing concert ticket resale markets](https://www.aftereod.com/archives/drljw91uv8bxg14imk6knccvm5ypte). This mini-project had two consequences: expectedly, it was my gateway project from casual Claude usage to use-Claude-Code-for-everything, and unexpectedly, it has tempted me into 'selling out'.

To analyse these WhatsApp-based resale markets, building tools to parse, clean and enrich chaotic WhatsApp group-chats was technically unconceivable to me 12 months ago, extremely frustrating 6 months ago but intoxicatingly simple last month. Ever since, I've begun to reimagine projects stuck in my 'ideas log' as quick 'CC sessions'. Is it making me 'dumber'? Idk. Is building stuff fun? Yes.

Strangely, writing this article made me confront a long-held belief of mine: that I would never make money from music (broadly culture, media, entertainment etc). Unto this point, I have chosen to separate my source of income from the few things I love, for fear that my feelings will be diluted by capitalism's wickedness. Working in or adjacent to music/culture was, until recently, 'selling out' to an unimaginable degree (even worse than....management consulting). This project's marriage of left and right brain, its punkness and its actual validity challenged this view at an opportune moment.

Coinciding with my aforementioned disenchantment towards the local music scene as well as sky-high conviction on the entertainment/culture/experience industry in India (the headline from 15ish months of Putt One), I plan to spend time over the next few months leaning into this 'sell-out' thesis.

#### Reflections two years on from starting this 'thingi'

This past year was the second year of writing this monthly newsletter thingis, and I was far less consistent than the year before. This reflects how I feel about writing these: they've now taken an amorphous shape, and I'm not sure what they are anymore. Every read comes off as more preachy and vain than the previous edition, and it continues to feel like a dance between performance and honesty.

To see how this continues to find its feet and eventually take some form, this is the first of many posts on Substack. I will continue to post this on Notion (and WhatsApp) too, yet I hope that the relative anonymity of Substack will help me write more freely.

Yet, writing this has continued to help me stay in touch with the people I care about in my life. Even better is reading the monthly updates of those who also write this. Long may it continue ![😄](<Base64-Image-Removed>)​

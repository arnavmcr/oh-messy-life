Smack dab in the middle of gigs at the Humming Tree in Bangalore and High Sprits in Pune, the F-16s graced the stage at Mumbai’s Hard Rock Cafe, amidst all the revelry that has the entire city preoccupied. The string of tour dates take them across the length and breadth of the country in support of their first full-length release, ‘Triggerpunkte’, in many regards the overdue seminal album of our generation. With their obscure teaser clips and massive singles, expectations ran high prior to the release of the album and the subsequent tour. It is safe to say that, on most fronts, the band has broken every conceivable glass ceiling.

What continues to be a tragically regular occurrence, the venue’s population comprised a hundred inebriated corporate job working onlookers who did the same dance regardless of the song, and a handful of individuals with the faintest of interest in the stellar performance they were witness to. Everything about the venue, from its awkward seating arrangement to its extremely un-hard rock playlist set a dichotomous tone for the night, with the band bringing to the fore their diverse, edgy and most importantly original music.

\[caption id="attachment\_383" align="alignnone" width="5918"\]![dsc_0031](/images/f16s-live-at-hard-rock-cafe-worli/dsc_0031.jpg) From L - R : Shank, Viraj, Josh, Vikram, Harsh\[/caption\]



As the F16s took to the stage, their bellicose was evident but they exuded professionalism as they played the entire set without the slightest murmur of discontent. The candour the band displayed spanned an hour and a half of the most delectable of tracks, some old and some new. ‘Avalanche’, the set opener revisited the band’s initial, raw sound which has, since, evolved significantly. Chronologically next were ‘Jacuzzi’ and ‘Blackboard’, which coincidentally, were from the band’s second release. The band fiddled with different renditions on Blackboard, but both songs were played without the usual panache the band is known for. The members looked slightly fatigued and perturbed, yet their cohesion was spectacular. Every synth arpeggio, slide and octave was played flawlessly. As the band proceeded to play their newer material, vocalist Josh’s (who, for lack of a better word, has the sexiest speaking voice there is) guitar fell victim to the exhausting tour schedule, with a string breaking. Even though he was back to his scintillating best within five minutes or so, it became increasingly conspicuous that the F16s live act would never be the same again.

What was once the pinnacle of live music is now veering with sordidness. ‘Triggerpunkte’ was an artistic triumph on every possible front – with music of massive scope. The sheer sonic magnitude of the album is incomprehensible and holds the highest reverence in every possible regard. However, unlike the band’s previous releases, ‘Triggerpunkte’ lacks when brought to the live frontier. Tracks like ‘Cannibal Life II’ and ‘Cadillak’ were pillars of a bold, eclectic sound the band were exploring, yet the intricacies that make the aforementioned tracks were subpar on the night. The fuzzy bass and the shimmering flourishes that were lacking were testament to a band that has perhaps pushed the envelope beyond their immediate reach. An over-reliance on samples, especially vocal ones, was disappointing and unexpected from a band that has mastered the act of crafting their sound.

\[caption id="attachment\_384" align="alignnone" width="6000"\]![dsc_0076](/images/f16s-live-at-hard-rock-cafe-worli/dsc_0076.jpg) Vocalist/guitarist Josh \[/caption\]

What set the band apart was their distinctive yet diverse sound – one that had blood, sweat and tears put into perfecting. The melange of guitar pedals juxtaposed a smooth, running bass tone and restrained drumming has been their most potent weapon. All of the above were at their optimum level, yet the layers of multiformity the band has embraced in their newer work did not meet the self-inflicted unbelievably high standard.

That being said, the discrepancies were unobtrusive. They were mired behind a curtain of five of our generation’s most talented musicians doing what they never fail to do – play the show of their lives. The performance was a worrying success, as the band flirts with the problems of scale while continuing to put out groundbreaking new music. Whether it was the paltry audience or the inevitable exhaustion, one can only hope that like Josh’s guitar, the band can be back to their best soon.

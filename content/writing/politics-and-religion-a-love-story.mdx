Marx once wrote, “Religion is the opiate of the people.” If only our comrade was alive and kicking today to witness the existential crises religion now faces. Religion as a concept has been kicked around, smothered, bent and broken and now faces its biggest, unprecedented opposition. This opposition is widely considered to be science and that perception has been ruthlessly romanticized (as has the notion of atheism), but is in fact not.

Politics is the single largest threat religion faces today.

The 18th Century saw the ‘Philosophical Enlightenment’ come to the forefront of society. The focus shifted away from the collective and to the individual. The need for rights rode a wave of popularity and was soon a pre-requisite for society. The post-Renaissance period saw momentous scientific development, with Europe being at the heart of this unprecedented progress.  The Industrial Revolution saw the advent of mass production and the mechanization of all possible articles – living or non-living.  The notion of monarchies began to fade away; apparently people did not appreciate that only a certain few people controlled a large number of others.  The intricate foundation for modern society was fabricated from these fragmented, seemingly unrelated events.

All in all, aesthetics were replaced by prosthetics. Prosthetic realms of society were synthesized in an attempt to satiate the mundane middle class. The populace whose lives were previously a mesh of respect for their labor, love for their neighbor and above all dedication and faith in their God was transformed into a sedentary, inhumane and monotonous life in the post-Industrial Revolution period. The Ruling Class had to, in some way, provide the proletariat with a purpose, and thus construed from thin air – the Modern State.

Religion began to be quite hastily wane, leaving mankind a race slightly baffled by its existence.

A bit of a side note here, it is assumed that the ultimate goal of the state is to ensure the safety and well-being of the population and the preservation of its unique culture. Concepts such as economic growth, military strength and infrastructural development may be indicators of the above but are not the immediate goals of the state. They are often bi-products, but should ideally never be the raison d’être of the state.

Flash forward two hundred years – two hundred bloody, war filled years polluted with abnormal advances in technology of all forms. You find yourself in 1947 – a watershed year in the history of the modern world. Japan is still ravaged with our first taste of American democracy, Nazi Germany is now a pawn in the United States’ and the USSR’s proxy war and the world everywhere is swept with the French Revolution’s ideals of ‘Liberty, Fraternity and Equality’. The gradual disintegration of empires began as the UN-era dawned upon the world. The UN, whose charter vehemently opposes colonialism of any form quite ironically, gave the Great Britain a Permanent Seat in its Security Council.

Side note - With the exception of maybe India, there exists no culturally heterogeneous state in our world. From the dominantly Catholic European states, to the Arab Middle East and Confucius China, there are a minute number of states that can be confidently dubbed as pluralistic, or whose population comprises large chunks of people from different cultures.

While all these developments are undoubtedly earth shattering, there are two events that took place in 1947casually overlooked that quite ominously occur almost simultaneously. Both events gave birth to three modern states, both of which are unlike any other.

First off, we find ourselves on the banks of the Mediterranean, where millions of Jews found themselves after being systematically annihilated by Nazi Germany. The Jews congregated from all walks of life and from all parts of the world at the site of their most sacred monuments in the Western-most part of the Middle East. This place, eponymous with the Biblical kingdom, is now known as Israel. 1947 saw the Zionists (Jewish nationalists) engage in military combat with the Arabs occupying the area, and eventually adopt the UN peace plan for the region which officially sanctioned the creation of Israel.

But what sets Israel aside from the pack is not that it is the only Jewish state in the world, and not that the Zionists revived a dead, unspoken language and converted into the mother tongue of the state. It is that despite being repeatedly eradicated by every major religion in this world, Israel and its Jewish still continues to be a pinnacle of success for the modern state. The incorporation of the intrinsic Jewish values into the daily lives of the population has been a key factor in the unprecedented rise of a state doomed to fail since its inception. The flawless amalgamation between religion and modernity that Israel has almost institutionalized has led to a society that has the ability to appreciate, honor and respect its rich heritage as well as look to the future as responsible, liberal citizens. From worship at the Wailing Wall, to being the Gay Capital of the Middle East, Israeli values evolve but never change. Modern history has been plagued with rampant Arab-Israeli conflict, but the fact of the matter is that Israel exists and will always exist because of its theocratic nature. The incorporation of religion into the state’s ideology and functioning itself is a key factor in determining its success.

Israel deserves a sovereign, Zionist state that extends from Egypt in the south to Lebanon in the north, with the Mediterranean blue and Syria and Jordan bordering it on the east. The United Nations continuously introduces resolution after resolution condemning, accusing, vilifying Israel, but yet they are the only state in the immediate surroundings that hasn’t faced political turmoil in the recent past. Israel continues to set the standard for Middle Easter economics, the perfect precedent of democracy with a poised balance between economic ideologies. Israel, or rather the Zionist state of Israel should be the sole legitimate government over this land. History, sociology and psychology dictate the redundancy of the presence of any race other than the Jews, who, as history has shown us, will always fight.

We now move geographically east and find ourselves in the Korean Peninsula, perhaps the only Cold War frontier that still exists today. The peninsula is home to two nations, both of which refuse to acknowledge the existence of the other – the Republic of Korea and the Democratic People’s Republic or Korea, or South Korea and North Korea. Previously administered  by the Japanese empire up until the end of the Second World War, the Peninsula was divided into two states solely based on economic ideology, with the North adopting a more leftist, socialist stance while the South leaning to the far-right. 1947 saw the outbreak of the Korean War with Sino- Soviet and UN forces pledging their allegiances to the North and the South respectively. Between the Korean War and today, we see the negative connotation associated with Communism show its ugliest form. The official ideology of the DPRK is _juche,_ a variant of Marxism-Leninism that prescribes self-reliance that was founded by Kim-Il-Sung, the first Supreme Leader of the State. Considered a’ hermit’ kingdom because of its isolation from the rest of the world, we find that the DPRK has stayed agonsingly devout to _juche_. The complete abstention from the ‘Western evils’ of capitalism is undoubtedly unique, but is also one of the reasons the world’s image and impression of the state is often demonic. The almost-criminalization of leftism has left the DPRK in a lonely place where it faces the hatred of the entire world for its economic ideology.

_Juche_ is often equated with the state religion, and its core tenants are often compared with Christianity. The relentless devotion the DPRK shows toward _juche_ is a remarkable example of a state prepared to go to any extent to preserve its beliefs. Unfortunately, the realization of these beliefs has not been able to successfully completed as repeated geographic instability coupled with mass international sanctions have crippled every Korean effort to realize its true potential. The DPRK is the country everyone loves to hate, and their fantastical military exercises, other-worldly palaces and their almost Orwellian dystopia makes it effortless to cry foul against them. Even while the country completely legally and indigenously developed nuclear weapons, sticking to _juche_, the world was compelled to heavily sanction their already faltering economy. Any threat to the current world order, especially to the Western capitalist world, is met with a brouhaha and wide scale disapproval and propaganda against the state. We saw this again with Iran in the 2010s and we will undoubtedly see it again in the future. While hatred for _juche_ is a different debate altogether, it cannot be denied that running a state on an unorthodox ideology and successfully administrating a country into rather astounding technological and military advancement is an achievement in itself.

Thus we observe that in today’s overly globalised yet desensitized world, the preservation of one’s belief system is close to impossible. The media, the government and the system are constantly coercing the individual into accepting ideals they believe are correct, often along the lines of the Western ideals of democracy.  Despite massive international pressure, a handful of states have managed to resist the duress. It is no coincidence that these states have used religion as the primary weapon in their arsenal to combat this duress.

The state as a standalone concept is flawed, as is religion but, only because man is flawed (Reinterpretation of an Angles and Demons quote). It is when the two get married that the world witnesses the beautiful union of two of its sworn rivals.

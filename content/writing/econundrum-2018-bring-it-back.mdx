Festival culture is a hallmark of Xavier's culture. The various faculties that exist used to operate under a laissez-faire-esque system, departments would have their own festivals with little-to-no interference from the administration, ensuring that they'd each have their day in the sun.

This did begin to change in 2016, with the administration realizing how hackneyed festivals had become simply because of their proliferation. There were just too many festivals taking places.

Unfortunately, Econundrum, the festival hosted by the Economics department felt the brunt of this - being relegated out of the permitted few. This made bringing it back in 2017 both a political and a logistical challenge.

Politically, it required lobbying the administration to let the festival happen. We needed buy-ins from the principal and the central authorities, as well the Department itself. Seemingly straightforward, these politics were definitional 'upward stakeholder management', corporatespeak for pacification. These multitudes of stakeholders had to see the festival fit their agenda.

Logistically, reviving an event after a two-year hiatus required improvisation, building momentum and setting up processes. The precedent for the festival barely existed - a handful of seniors reminiscing of days long lost was all we had to build off. This necessitated that we had to start from scratch - creating events, reaching out to speakers and managing teams.

The Quartet was responsible less for operational execution and more for the aforementioned stakeholder management. Top-down ideation for things like the vision for our year, the values we imbibed as a festival and ensuring teams were sufficiently communicated with were other things we looked after. It was a great primer in compromise and negotiating tricky decisions - something I that I increasingly grateful for after navigating the corporate world.

As vice-chairperson for Events, my responsibilities included recruiting a team that would conceptualize, organise and execute a host of various events. These would be of several different genres, some of them testing technical participants' knowledge of economics, others applying economics concepts to real-life situations.

\[caption id="attachment\_795" align="alignnone" width="1040"\]![26233503_1228309630635443_1823617428139490484_o](/images/econundrum-2018-bring-it-back/26233503_1228309630635443_1823617428139490484_o.jpg) Mock Stock - an event that tests players' negotiation skills and financial acumen\[/caption\]

The festival itself was a resounding success. We recorded the highest footfall in the festival's 12+ year history and had participants come in from across the country. All ten events went off flawlessly and (eventually) the staff and workforce was able to learn and grow from the festival.

Econundrum 2018 was a personal success as it reinforced my penchant for pushing the envelope and doing 'zero to one'. It gave me immense creative freedom to set up processes, and subsequently a high-standard for editions to come. It taught me patience and restraint when dealing with the authorities, and empathy and mindfulness when managing teams.

\[caption id="attachment\_750" align="alignnone" width="1040"\]![26198383_1227140257419047_6505604417009537839_o](/images/econundrum-2018-bring-it-back/26198383_1227140257419047_6505604417009537839_o.jpg) Smiling because of the banter on the walkie\[/caption\]

It was quite an honour when I was called to judge the Lalit Doshi Memorial debate of the 2020 edition of Econundrum. It was, similar to Malhar, a sense of ownership and legacy over the festival. Seeing familiar faces, being given the same instructions I had given to judges on countless occasions, and watching the participants make the same snarky comments I did when I debated was heartwarming - it felt like I was coming back home.

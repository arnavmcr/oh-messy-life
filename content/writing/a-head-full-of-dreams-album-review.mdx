_**A review of Coldplay's 2015 album 'A Head Full of Dreams'. Original can be found [here.](http://www.chordsunited.com/blog/reviews/2/19/1/coldplay-head-full-of-dreams-album-review.html)**_

The album opens with the eponymous A Head Full of Dreams, which begins with faint drizzling synths that have played such a frequent role in Coldplay songs in the recent past. The bass-line immediately draws your attention and keeps it there for the rest of the song. In typical Coldplay fashion, the song has the sing-alongable ‘Ooooh Aaah’ sections, which lead you into a frantic and climatic end. The song sets the tone for the entire album, with its unique melody and definite live appeal. The album opener would likely be used as a set-opener by the band, with its parabolic structure that alleviates the listener almost instantly. Next up, ‘Birds’, is a departure to a more familiar Coldplay sound. The heavily delayed and reverberated guitars and Martin’s surreal falsetto are like two peas in a pod, giving the listener a very care-free, joyous experience. The post chorus sees the band shine in all respects, with a long, almost riotous yet jubilant build-up that culminates rather abruptly.

![ahfod](/images/a-head-full-of-dreams-album-review/ahfod.gif)

“Hymn for the Weekend” takes off from where Birds left off, with a rather eerie and awkwardly used sample giving way for the familiar four chord Coldplay piano patter we love. The song hits all the right notes musically, with the phenomenal use of violins and other effects. Lyrically, unfortunately, this song does not make the cut as the lyrics are rather immature and very uncharacteristic of the band. The arrangement of this song makes for an Arena-Rock song, and will also appeal to younger, newer Coldplay fans with its decadent lyrics and dancey feel. The big-hitting guitar riffs and basslines are traded up for the piano on ‘Everglow’. Reminiscent of the ballads the band has written previously, ‘Everglow’ is a very potent addition to this list. The track in its entirety has an ethereal feel to it, with the delayed guitar solo and falsetto sonically transporting the listener to a very calming place. The song could definitely act as a substitute for a set-closer, with its light music and morning-after, sober feel. All in all, one of the strongest songs on the album that will leave all fans – old and new – satisfied.

Jumping right back into the fray, “Adventure of a Lifetime”, the first single of the album, has all the makings of a modern day ‘indietronic’ anthem. The verse and the chorus will be met with the inevitable head bobbing, and the superficial lyrics create a joyous, exuberant atmosphere. Adventure of a Lifetime will undoubtedly find its way into Summer Mixes and Coldplay’s set lists with its celebratory vibe. A characteristic of modern music not commonly seen with bands – collaborations and features – finds its way onto an unlikely location, with Tove Lo playing her part on “Fun”, the next track.  The synth works on the album, although it is rather uncharacteristic of the band. Tove Lo’s presence on the album seems pointless and irrelevant as she gets no real place or time to really make her mark on the album or even on the track for that matter. Her voice acts as a harmony to Martins’ and does not do much to supplement it. The most out-of-place track on the album, “Kaleidoscope”, sees the band attempt to transform themselves into a pseudo-political band, which Coldplay is not. The U2-esque status is one that Coldplay can never and will never be able to achieve, and any attempt at the same would be counter-productive. The sample has no real relevance in the album, and its message which is not really consistent and lacks the maturity that the sample communicates. This track is preachy, unnecessary and takes away more than it gives. Up next, “Army of One”, opens with the use of samples with subtle distorted bass sonically enhancing the song. The first segment of the song is rather plain, lacking the lyrical or musical fervor to really stand out. As the first segment fades, a darker feel to the song emerges, with the chord sequence and the drums reminiscent of Kendrick Lamar’s ‘Swimming Pools’. This segment shows us a whole new dimension of the band’s musical escapade, with dreary, juvenile lyrics and heavily modified and synthesized samples and effects. This’ experiment’ of sorts falls rather flat on its face, with the  song sounding like a modern day rap song with its trashy lyrics and auto-tuned harmonies, not to mention the complete departure of Chris Martin’s unbelievable vocal talent to a deep, monotonous tone.

Coming back to the more positive lyrics and music that defines the band’s recent albums, ‘Amazing Day’, is a mellow sounding track that once again has a very sober feeling. A layered, soothing journey this song takes you on is refreshing and culminates in a massive, euphoric breakdown of-sort, one that will undoubtedly sit well with their live act. The second interlude of sorts, “Color Spectrum”, is once again unnecessary. It adds nothing to the album, and has no real value. “Up&Up” is an immaculate representation of the ‘new Coldplay’, with its poignant lyrics and staggering yet minimal use of electronics. The electronics are few and far between and the live instruments run the show, with the vocals taking center stage. The post-chorus guitar solo is a showcase for the instrumentalists of the band, often neglected due to Martin’s on stage antics and presence. Another sober, exciting track on an album that has given us many to choose from, “Up&Up” is a lyrical and musical triumph on an album that may not have been either.

Described by Martin himself, Coldplay’s “hippie album” proves to be a lukewarm addition to the bands otherwise stellar range of albums. For a band renowned as one of the best live acts on the planet, the album strays a little from what may be in their best interest live. It is very likely that a host of on-stage additions will be present on the upcoming tours, taking away from the charm of watching Coldplay live. The transition into a very accessible electronic rock atmosphere will disappoint sections of the Coldplay fan base and once again the album keeps us wondering where the band goes next – in a more negative than positive way.

In a time where band’s are always looking to distinguish themselves from the current crop of bands, Coldplay fail to do that, with their legacy carrying them on for the better part of this album. Having said this, quality music can be found on this release, if you look hard enough. If only it sounded like the Coldplay we love.

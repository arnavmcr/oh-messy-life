_**A review of Bombay based singer/songwriter Awkward Bong's record 'In the Brightest Corners'.**_

Whilst our scene has a plethora of singer-songwriters, with their soothing guitar hooks and sappy lyrics, Awkward Bong seem to transcend this (or any) tag. The live manifestation of tunes that Ronit Sarkar (vocals, songwriting, and guitar) has garnered over his years both as a photographer and a musician is, Awkward Bong.

Their first full length release, “In the Brightest Corners”, takes you on a sonic journey that revitalises opinion and calms the senses. Pop sensibility coupled with sporadic electronic takes leaves the listener with a sense of longing, nostalgia and warmth. The album does not have a central theme per se, but traverses from the oh-so-typical yet atypical love songs to his desire to get away from the city Sarkar calls home.

The record opens with the track “Spaceship”, where we are first introduced to the ubiquitous entity that is the voice of Sarkar. With its undeniable sing-alongability, Spaceship makes for a very sensible set-opener and acts as a precursor for what is to come.  The smooth baritone of the vocals layered with synthesized melodies and restrained guitar playing embody not just the song, but the entire album.



The track instantly descends into “Waste the Night Away”, a song showcasing the necessity and utility of a live-act, with bass lines and lead sections that weave their way into your mind long after you’ve drifted away into a state of mind that has been fabricated by Awkward Bong. The sing-alongability trend has carried on with a chorus of Sarkar’s falsettos proving to be the highlight of the song.  The bridge section sees the use of samples, a technique that has been employed often but whose intention seems rather ambiguous. Nevertheless, its adds yet another layer to the song and serves as a platform for the build-up that culminates in a half-time breakdownesque part with haunting harmonies.

The next song,”Burning Up”, changes the direction of the sound a little, going toward a more stripped down, raw place. While the acoustic guitar sound provides a solid base for the song to go forward from, it is the ingenuity of the songwriting and the lush guitar sound, rife with delay and reverb that take this track to the ‘album highlight’ level. The proficiency of the guitarist is showcased in a deviously crafted solo that is held together by the bass notes, giving the listener a peaceful, heart warming section.

The record once again takes a drastic turn, leading into the couple tracks of the record – “Step”, and “Step Hard”. In come the piano and the vocal chops that tease the listener before a distorted, bass driven build up that transport the sound to a heavy, unfiltered place where the sheer rawness and emotion of the vocals come through. The song is indeed very catchy with its 4/4 simplicity but coerces the listener to tap their foot and headbang with every note. The song crescendos to a point with an intentionally off timed guitar solo smothered with effects, pounding drums and samples till the piano kicks in to deceive for a short section after which the song returns to its fervent sound.

“Let Me Out, Let Me Go”, a satire on Bombay, the city that has no ‘real winter ‘and is an unassailable crowd favourite, mainly because of it’s opening verse that humorously speaks of the City’s terrible weather and pollution. As always, the subtle layers begin creeping in, with a second guitar, the bass supplementing the vocals sumptuously. The intricacies of the songwriting id highlighted in this song with the use of metaphors masked behind the music.

The first single off the album, “Golden” packs a punch from the get go, with its quintessential indie rock flavour coupled. The arpeggiated guitars blended with the the elusiveness of the protagonist in the story leave the listener grasping for more with each listen. The distinctiveness of the guitar tone itself intrigues, with a bridge that has vocals swaying from octave to octave followed by a soundscape plastered with all four intrsuments at once creating a unique congregation that has every part of the spectrum of frequencies satiated.

“You Don’t Have To Hide” balances out the energy and chaos of “Golden” with its downtempo drums and gentle piano that flows into the reverby guitars that echo throughout the song. Once again, the vocalists’ falsetto shines through while he pines and promises with his words. While the melodies and sections may not be the most musically complex, it is their simplicity that elevate the sound. Creative restraint is a trait that has been mastered by this band and they have moulded a sound that works rather beautifully in a live setting as well as on the record.

Finally comes album closer “You Haunt Me’, indicative of the album’s effect on any listener. Lush synths and sporadic guitar give this track a nostalgic, reminiscent feel that once again assert the sing-alongability that the band never fail to provide. Probably the ‘hands wave in the air’ song off the album, this song is a testament to ‘less is more’. Its minimalism and simplicity are redolent of the opener, “Spaceship”but by the time the entire album has been processed and taken in, it is unlikely that the listener will be in the same mental place.

 

Undoubtedly a highlight of the scenes pop-rock albums of the year, “In the Brightest Corners” is a coalescent collection of warm, cosy tracks that delve into realms of your mind and stay there. It cannot be questioned that Awkward Bong are one of the most dynamic acts present today and if this album is any indicator as to the enormous potential that hides behind Sarkar’s baby face, we are all in for a treat.

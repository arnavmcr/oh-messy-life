Malhar is often used interchangeably with religion in Marx's hackneyed quote - "Religion is the opiate of the masses. It is the soul of a soulless world."

Malhar holds a special place in the heart of Xavier's alumni - temporarily alleviating us from the remainder of our college mundanity. It encapsulates all the positive traits the college has to offer - camaraderie, loyalty and creativity being at the fore of this festival.

In true Malhar spirit, I did my time as a volunteer and finally moved up the ranks. I idolized my Seniors, in awe of their composure, brevity and charisma, and associated Malhar with the pinnacle of my Xavier's tenure - my "position" being indicative of the success or failure at the college.

While these materialistic, immature streams of thought died down, my intention to make my mark in Malhar history did not. As an Organizer part of the Conclave department, my responsibilities included speaker curation and management, as well as conceptualizing and executing an event.

Our Conclave session was the first that hosted a "Performance Session". As someone with a deep passion for music, I fought vehemently for this to be included. It would allow me to use my knowledge and networks to bring musicians to a wider audience. This motivation led me to push for a full-blown concert performance in college, rather than just an intimate, sit-down performance. This too would be the first in the festival's history, and always had strong opposition from the staff due to its feasibility.

Fast forward three months after these initial conversations, and we had hosted Divine (of Gully Boy fame) at Malhar, both as a speaker (where he narrated heart-wrenching, honest narratives - I have only heard, I was manning the doors fighting off manic fans), and as a performer in front of few thousand students. It was, without a shadow of a doubt, a defining moment in Malhar's history and has set a ridiculously high standard for editions to come.

His set was one for the books - forty minutes of energy, panache and honesty. It was also, as far as I know, the introductory performance for Raja Kumari (who has been doing bits as well) in India. It was incredible as a standalone concert, but when put in the context of it being a college festival with no tickets and no payment to the artist, it was unbelievable.

Amidst this fanfare, the memory of that day I have etched in my head was standing outside college, smoking a cigarette with Divine. We had a great conversation about hip-hop music, music culture in Bombay, and his fans. It struck a deeper chord with me because I recall watching Divine years before his performance at Malhar, and nothing much had changed (yet). His verses were still as tongue-in-cheek as ever, and his delivery as cutting as always. And yet he had gone from performing in small venues as a teenager, to an icon whose lyrics were on the tongue of every student in that quadrangle. Following that, in a matter of weeks, he was headlining festivals where he used to have an opening slot, and in a matter of months was the subject of a seminal movie in Bollywood history. Yet, there he was - doing something my friends and I did on a daily basis through college - sitting at a tapri, drinking chai and talking about music. A role model both on and off the stage.

 

I will not feign humility - I am regularly irked by people who say "scene kya hai" or "apna time aayega" as I feel a sense of ownership over Divine and his music. In that moment, however, when he said "aawaz karo public", the fact that I had seen him perform over ten times, or the fact that I organized his performance did not matter. There was a collective catharsis from every individual at the festival - the months of toil that had gone into putting on this show were absolved in those forty minutes of hedonism.

The other aspect of my Malhar journey was far less romantic but equally rewarding. My lasting passion for football and penchant for statistics and economics manifested themselves in an even I designed and executed, blandly titled "Simulation" at the time. The event ran on Microsoft Excel and used more descriptive statistics than my 6 semesters in college did. It borrowed concepts from finance and some of the work I had done in actuarial science, but at the end of the day the reason I hold it in such high regard is because of the team I ran it with.

\[caption id="attachment\_746" align="alignnone" width="1365"\]![20819010_1599632233414786_1803982422576656994_o](/images/malhar-2017-a-new-level/20819010_1599632233414786_1803982422576656994_o.jpg) Our auctioneer at work with the team in the background\[/caption\]

My team of 10 "lads" were definitely not the most technically astute, nor did they have some extraordinary industry. What they did have, however, was a sense of camaraderie that extended across the team. To this day, the number of "Simulation" friendships and relationships that developed is something I had overlooked completely while designing the event. Its success wasn't measured by how fast, or how well we could pull it off, but by the lasting bonds that were developed within that group - an unparalleled sense of trust, loyalty and collectivism.

\[caption id="attachment\_748" align="alignnone" width="2048"\]![21167580_1677556095601497_7566380094386187248_o](/images/malhar-2017-a-new-level/21167580_1677556095601497_7566380094386187248_o.jpg) One of the "lads" (L) and me (R), clearly hard at work\[/caption\]

Whether it was writing nicknames on the back of everyone's t-shirt like professional footballers do, betting on participants success, or trying to get Divine and his crew to stop smoking in the college staffroom, our allegiances were both to each other, as well as the festival. We did our jobs and had fun while doing them.

Perhaps it was only life coming full-circle then, when the following year our band played the penultimate slot at AmNight, a concert for the Malhar workforce. While AmNight is short for Amateurs Night, our band barely scratched the surface of amateur - with our stolen guitars and broken drum-kits. Our setlist (which in true local musician spirit, we wrote down and taped to the stage) comprised covers of bad pop songs we knew the crowd would love. The set was a culmination of five years in Xavier's, so the emphasis was less on technical skills and more on nostalgia. Inappropriate references to ex-girlfriends, two-man mosh pits and summoning "special guests" (our only slightly less musically gifted friends) - we made the most of our fifteen minutes of fame. A fitting end to our Xavier's careers.

When I hear about Malhar, it immediately evokes a plethora of vivid imagery - blood, sweat and tears, the whole shebang. It was an undoubtedly formative experience in my life, and will forever hold a fond place in my heart.

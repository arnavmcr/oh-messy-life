Unlike most places I've written about or documented in the past, Jakarta is somewhat of an outlier. It is an inflexion point in the motive that is at the core of my travels. From being a student who was fortunate enough to see as much of the world as I could over the past five years or so, I had to channelise this wide-eyedness as a supposed working professional in Jakarta, something I'm still in disbelief about.

Disbelief is something that characterised every part of this journey - from the day I got the job and the prospect of working in a foreign land to successfully cooking my first grilled cheese sandwich in my apartment in Jakarta, there was always underlying surrealism that persisted. And it still does.

Jakarta was very much a city well on its feet. Like most metropolis' and like the hackneyed romanticisation of their stark inequality and visibility, Jakarta is no exception. Glistening skyscrapers adjacent to shantytowns and all the capitalist fairytales that come with such a setting occupy much of the narrative across these giant urban agglomerations. What sets my experiences with Jakarta apart from those I have had in similar mega-cities is primarily the volume of routineness I was able to imbibe - giving me, in my opinion, a far more veracious account of my environment.

Perhaps the most discernible feature of the city was the inherent, for lack of a better word, niceness of the people. It was a welcome change from the perpetual disregard for one's surroundings Bombay had made me accustomed to. This is in no way taking away from the grittiness, drive and ferocity of the city - Jakarta has all of that and then some. Yet this intensity does not come at the cost of patience, and an unparalleled eagerness to help.

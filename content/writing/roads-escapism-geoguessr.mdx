_“I love the city, I love the night; they’re always around” — the Lightyears Explode_

Perhaps the most neglected universal that forms the fabric of cities we live in and places we love, roads, are the most revealing of all. Their subtlety, their innocuousness and their omnipresence are all part of their charm, and on closer examination, are a wonderful cross-section of any city.

If there is a single encapsulation of life in Bombay, it would be sitting on the footpath in the wee hours of the morning with a glass of Boost, watching the party-goers, the milkman and the degenerate kids in their parents’ car congregate (no guesses for which category I was in), in a starkly universal yet inequitable reality. The liberation of walking home under streetlights and having to dodge the inevitable holes in the footpath made up a large chunk of my “me-time”, and was a feature wherever I’d travel. These walks soon became more than their function — allowing me to imbibe my surroundings and truly engage with the tarmac I was walking on and the constructions it supported. _I must also, from the outset, acknowledge the privilege I have of being able to walk the streets of Bombay (and most parts of the world) without being afraid for my personal safety._

I’m not sure when this fascination with roads was born, but I distinctly recall my mum patiently trying to convey to me that the camera’s memory card was full and she’d have to delete some of the hundreds of street signs that I’d constantly been taking pictures of while we were on vacation in Singapore. These signs donned every corner of every street in places we’d visit, and would have different colours and different typography — I was smitten! While the etymology of street names escaped me in those days, just their aesthetics and meticulousness had me, like any normal 12 year old, gleaming.

Ever since roads and the life they breed have become an essential part of my understanding of cities. From Flushing Avenue in Bushwick to the mighty Setiabudi in Jakarta, the cathartic act of aimless wandering on streets and observing the oddities that make the road what it is was one of the most honest ways of interacting with cities. Perhaps stemming from a Debordian exploration of the psychogeography of these cities, there was a wonderful resistance that walking gave me. In some ways this liberation stemmed from a rejection of the efficiency-driven world of modern transport and a proclamation of faith in the pace of my own two feet that often gave the remainder of my senses sufficient time and space to digest its surroundings. The street food, the assortment of stickers plastered on signals and the flyers and foliage that became souvenirs were all the additional perks of these moments of “insurgency”.

However, the most alluring part of this temporary vagrancy is its infiniteness — every time I’d reach the end of the road, there was another one I could walk on and then another. It never ended and, if anything, only compounded with time. Unlike my other outlets of escapism — music, sport and food — which were decidedly finite, these walks had no end and no agenda. This limitlessness of my surroundings was a realisation that was unexpected and liberating beyond belief, and my ability to traverse these surroundings were constrained only by my own faculties.

As I found myself unable to travel after months of it being second nature and an inextricable part of my life, that feeling of escapism that travel, and unhinged urban exploration as a large subset of that, provided seemed to evaporate almost instantly — those limitless excursions suddenly became institutionally prohibited. And while there were activities that demanded unintended mindfulness, like cooking, the excitement, mystery and insight that my beloved roads brought with them were hard to find. That is until we discovered [GeoGuessr](http://geoguessr.com) — the panacea to my travel-less disgruntlement.

The premise of the game is simple — you are presented with a random Google Street View from somewhere across the world and are expected to identify the place. The closer you were to the actual spot, the more points you get. The interface is very simple — you can pan, zoom and move around for as long as Google Street View permits, and making a selection is dead easy. What is not-so-easy is attempting to decipher the difference between Thai, Khmer and Laotian scriptures, making mental notes of countries that drive on the left or the right of the street and inevitably mistaking a .cl domain extension for Colombia, and not Chile.

![The wonderful GG interface](/images/roads-escapism-geoguessr/05Up-i-1n5tSrN7mA.webp)

The game spawned endless afternoons spent making the most of Zoom’s screenshare feature, and a collaborative effort among some of my other geography-inclined, wiltless friends. We’d spend hours traversing through the steppes of Central Asia yearning for the random city we saw on a signboard 20 minutes ago with an honest and unexpected level of penance. Everyone would be glued to the screen and armed with an extreme level of focus aimed at spotting the smallest of road signs, the colours of a number plate (the numbers themselves are usually blurred out) or the kind of vegetation we were surrounded with. And on we’d go to the next destination — the larger-than-life nature of Patagonia, the dense forests of somewhere in Kalimantan and the gentrified cobble stone streets of Italy were all options, as were pretty much all other mental images of all other countries and landscapes you can imagine. The liberation of walking through streets across the world and observing their idiosyncrasies was addicting. The same markers of a street that you’d notice while walking — food stalls, stickers, sign-boards and outfits are all visible as they would otherwise be, with the added benefit of their alteration at will.

Beyond the geography-flexes, learning about new places and the nuances of their language, my love for GeoGuessr (or GG, as we affectionately call it) is, like in the case of streets, its infiniteness. Both in terms of the number of views that exist (I don’t think there’s ever been one that’s repeated) and the seemingly never ending scrolling within each view, GeoGuessr isn’t addicting as much as it is consuming. Those afternoons were less lazy and more actively engaged with the slow exploration of a corner of the world we could only dream of visiting, and then another, and then another.

The coalescence and absorption of you by your surroundings is a concept that is not novel; carpe diem, mindfulness and focus are all commonly used words and phrases that refer to this unity. Yet this occurrence, once again in a Debordian sense, has become harder to come by. Roads, GeoGuessr and their limitlessness are two sources of this escapism.

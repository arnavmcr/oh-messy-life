As the local music industry realizes its true potential, it is unsurprising that malls are cashing out on this surge in popularity, which is what ‘Awestrung’, held at the Phoenix Mills did. The monthly event has given the likes of Laxmi Bomb, the Colour Compound and Sandunes, amongst others, a unique platform to showcase their novel musicality. The April edition saw three bands of local lineage congregate to put on a memorable show in an unlikely environment.  The lineup comprised ‘masala-punk’ band the Lightyears Explode, alternative metallers Blakc and a group of lazy Dadar boys, better known as Zero.

It was a little after seven that the Lightyears took the stage, their first show after formally departing with their drummer Aaron Carvalho. His replacement was the ferociously able Jeremy D’Souza, formerly of Goddess Gagged, who brought with him a plethora of experience. As a self-confessed Lightyears Explode groupie, expectations loomed large over the reformed lineup, but as the trio assembled themselves there was a foreboding sense of fatigue present.

The band rushed through their first few songs, playing ‘Revenge of Kalicharan’ and other songs off the eponymous album. Frontman Saurabh, who is usually the epitome of calmness, seemed a little bit frazzled, which exhibited itself during ‘Late Night Song’, where he invited Dinkar Dwivedi (of Bone Broke) to handle guitar duties. The band played half the song bereft of a capo which led to a noticeably empty performance, but cometh the hour, cometh the man. Tejas Menon (of Tejas) procured a capo that rescued both the song and the set. From then on the set was smooth sailing, with Dinkar and Saurabh handling guitar duties and Shalom and Jeremy handling the rhythm section. The band was at its explosive best, with their usual lovable lyrics and infectious riffs resonating with the crowd. ‘Kunj Gutka’ and ‘I Am a Disco Dancer’ capped off a rollercoaster performance, that posed serious questions about the tightness that was synonymous with the band, but reaffirmed that regardless of the lineup, the chemistry among the band members will be sempiternal.

Up next was Blakc, a band that destroyed every modicum of apprehension I had before the performance. Dressed in all black, the five piece brought their diverse brand of heavy tracks to the fray. Loud, distorted but never jarring, the band’s impeccable technique and dexterity blew away the audience. Their setlist brought together songs from both their earlier catalogue as well as their more recent ones. The band had an effortless grasp over every note and riff they played – with special emphasis on their ability to use effects on their guitars to synthesize instruments that significantly enhanced their sound. Both guitarists,  Reinhardt Dias and Anish Menon, integrated seamlessly, trading solo duties from time to time as well as thoughtfully adding layers to the band’s performance. Varun Sood, on the drums, used his large stature to command the rhythym section and also add intricate fills. The standout instrumentalist of the band, was bassist Roop Thomas. His vast array of technical knowledge coupled with his control over the stage and his ability to fill sonic space effortlessly, added an extremely well thought out and subtle dimension to the performance. Vocalist Shawn Pereira shone in the spotlight, with his octave bending notes and sharp lyrics adding the final piece to the puzzle. His Daniel Tompkins-esque vocal patterns coupled with his Zack de la Rocha style narration, showed his incredible versatility. The band demonstrated why they are stalwarts of alternative music in Bombay, with their guitar tapping and banging double bass imprinted in the minds of the audience for time immemorial.

After the heavy duty riffs and intense lyrics of Blakc, an army of characters assembled themselves into the crowd. A heightened sense of expectation was evident, but unlike the feeling before other shows in the city, this had an air of jubilation, even before the band was on stage. The entire crowd was Standing By, of course, for Zero.

Zero is the kind of band whose greatness lies in their simplicity, their innate nature to put on the show of their lives regardless of place or time. Yes, the hype was built up as this show was dubbed as the first formal reunion show, with radio interviews, newspaper articles and the like, but the gig was, at its core, a Zero gig. A gig where four friends come together to, as vocalist Rajeev Talwar said, ‘have some fun and let you guys join in on it’. The audience was rapturous as the band took the stage, with every word of every song being bellowed by the audience.

The cover laden set saw alt-rock classics such as ‘Vaseline’ by STP and ‘Hard to Handle’ by the Black Crowes interrupted by the hilarious antics of vocalist Talwar, who was  dictating rhymes and one-liners to the audience, who were clearly in splits. The arrangement for his travel to Bombay for the show from his residence in the United Kingdom was made evident through his hatred for the heat and his impersonation of the British accent. Bassist Girish Talwar noted, in a reference to Fire and Ice, the band has grown from playing the smallest of venues to now being institutionalized all over the country.  As the set progressed, the audience moved further in their evident ecstasy, uninhibitedly chanting the lyrics to ‘Lucy’ and subsequently ‘Come Together’ by the Beatles. Drummer Sid Coutto then announced a dedication to the late Prince, which preceded a set of songs off their 2005 album ‘Procrastination’. As the night grew older, the mosh pit began claiming its victims. ‘Not My Kind of Girl’, an ode to an out of sorts relationship sent a manic crowd into a frenzy. Next came a showcase for the mammoth guitarist that is Warren Mendonsa, with his fingers dancing across the fretboard relentlessly for the duration of ‘Christmas in July’, which amalgamated into a song that has defined a multitude of generations’ association with the local music scene.   ‘PSP 12”’ with its pounding drums and slap bass intro, had begun – almost like a ritual. As the song crescendoed toward the chorus, the excitement peaked, with any fiber of inhibition being lost as Talwar sang the words ‘Standing By’. The mosh pit engulfed the crowd. As Zero are a band of a different stature, so was the mosh pit – the typical participants of the pit were replaced by working,  shirt and tie clad professionals, couples ballroom dancing and a general sense of inexplicable joy that transcended all barriers. A quick glance at the pit led me to see a friend of mine piggy backing on someone else, and when I called out to him he replied saying – ‘Standing By’. Absolute hysteria plagued the crowd. The song was not just a performance, but was a celebration of humankind; a party like none other was therapeutic to another degree. Just when it seemed like the show was over, the band played the extremely apt and classic ‘Banana Boat’ by Harry Belafonte and capped off the night with the evergreen ‘La Bamba’. The show was a reminder to all present that quality trumps quantity, and that regardless of if Zero play one, twelve or even zero shows in a year, they are the benchmark of showmanship and performances, and will always be.

It was heartening to see the reach and popularity of an event such as this, and with the funds available to an organization such as High Street Phoenix; it can only grow bigger from here. As Bobby Talwar reminded us, the dearth of open air shows in the city is worrying, and it is a plea on behalf of the entire community that events such as this continue as they are imperative for the blossoming of this industry.

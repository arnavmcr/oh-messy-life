There are few memories as evocative as that of the backdrop a larger-than-life Jakarta skyline against bustling warungs that have “Bandung”, “Solo”, “Aceh” plastered across their headboard. The warungs are the foundation of Jakarta — the heart and soul of the city’s millions of residents, and the epitome of the melting pot of “Indonesian” culture if there ever were such a narrow, convenient epitome. Warungs, or street food carts, bring dishes and owners from across the country to their patrons, cooked fresh for your consumption. And warung meals, to me, were as much about the food as they were about escapism — taking a moment to distract yourself from the mundanity and monotony of our realities and being one with your surroundings. They allowed me to listen to families catch up about their day, and friends joking with each other (though my very limited understanding of Bahasa) as I ate my ayam bakar and drank my teh-o. It was the only time I felt connected with the context I was surrounded by, which is why street food, and food as a whole, to me, is much larger than itself. It is the fabric of our society.

With this spirit of adventure and escapism in mind, [koi](http://www.cookwithkoi.com) was born. We want to bring not just delicious ingredients and recipes, but also stories, love and memories to our community. At koi, we strongly believe in a slower-moving world where we can appreciate cultures different from our own, and spend an evening as if we were a part of that world — eat the same kind of food, listen to the same kind of music and learn about their history. Cultural relativism and its inherent link with food is something that koi holds as one of its founding tenets, and is something we strive to uphold throughout our experience.

From a more operational (read: boring) point of view, koi was something I thought of in late-May when I realised that I’m not going to be able to travel for the foreseeable future, and I was really craving Hor Fun from the stall on Purvis Street in Singapore, my nightly dinner for a considerable part of the past year. I wished there was a way for me to eat those meals while I was thousands of miles away. I asked my ramen and noodle connoisseur friend Aaron if he knew of any way I could eat this, and one thing led to another and a couple of weeks later, koi was born!

We started by getting legal clearance and adhering to basic safety standards, after which we used our rudimentary design skills to get our website and Instagram page up-and-running. From the outset, we wanted koi to reflect our understanding of food which was through watching food documentaries, being honest with our ingredients and understanding the context surrounding dishes. Our website and social media aim at showcasing these values.

With little expectation, we launched our inaugural Thai menu on our Instagram page. We adopted a once-a-month delivery model because we believe that good things are worth waiting for, and it is the only way we can ensure that our ingredient wastage is minimised. Another factor that we took into account was the fact that both Aaron and I are working day-jobs, and to meet the high-quality standards that we set for koi, mainly in terms of the freshness of ingredients, this model was the perfect fit. It also exuded our commitment to a slower-moving world and ensuring that our sole focus for the month is ensuring that we can learn about a new culture, and share our learnings with a single-minded focus.

Our first month was overwhelming, to say the least. We are immensely grateful to our friends and families who supported us by placing orders, but the sheer joy when we got an order from someone we had never met or interacted with before was unparalleled — a truly surreal feeling. Right from planning out how to pack boxes in our kitchens, to optimising delivery routes and writing newsletters, we had a month with an unbelievably steep learning curve across the board. In true college-student fashion, we extracted as much value from free tools as possible.

![captionless image](/images/building-koi/1gKIRom19gAwg_-lDmCfLfQ.jpeg)![Before & After: Learning how to take photographs from my influencer mother](/images/building-koi/1JhwMfBYgcDFFiRaP3NixIw.jpeg)

Our suite of tools includes Airtable at its core. A powerful and versatile product, Airtable offers a comprehensive free plan that serves most of our purpose. Everything from managing inventory, to recording orders and accounts, Airtable is our go-to “nerve centre”.

![What holds koi together](/images/building-koi/0I3Rx7406X8krsGJJ.png)

After a chaotic month 1 of using WhatsApp to communicate, we realised that there were just too many messages getting lost in the black box that WhatsApp is, so we moved our communications to Discord. We were already quite comfortable with using Discord through our multiple games of the board game Risk where we’d use that for our comms, so it seemed like an obvious choice. We’re contemplating moving to Slack’s free plan, which is definitely the industry standard for internal communications.

With Mailchimp, we were facing issues with bypassing the “Promotions” tab that we were being constantly finding our emails in, so we used Mailchimp to design our newsletters, but then exported this to an HTML file and sent it via a MailMerge plugin on Google Sheets. Since the free version has a very conservative ceiling, we use two separate Mail Merge plugins to send out our emails.

A big problem we faced was accounting for petty cash expenses we’d make at grocery stores, delivery executives or printers. In order to solve this problem on the go, we used Zapier to connect Google Tasks to Airtable. Tasks provides a simple interface that is accessible and easy-to-use on the go, and with a little bit of structuring, we were able to pull our spends from Tasks straight to Airtable, where the rest of our spends were being tracked.

Finally, for tracking orders, we have built an automation that _eases_ the transition between Wix, where we host our website, and Airtable, with a few macros on Google Sheets in the middle. We’re looking to do the same to track inventory as well.

As we moved on to our second month (and second city), we brought on Devdatta and Nyla to the team, who were instrumental in making our Viet Nam edition the resounding success it was. Devdatta, our in-house recipe expert, ensured that our recipes were of the highest quality, and also managed a large part of our email identity (along with generally keeping the rest of us “oldies” on our feet). Nyla leads our Bangalore operations and helps manage our social media presence. We were fortunate to have the expertise of our friend Anant who, with his encyclopedic knowledge of all things history, ensured that we stuck true to our promise of educating our audience. His support over the months has been incredible.

koi is our way of travelling, learning, and bringing people together. Each recipe we curate, each postcard we send and each ingredient we buy takes us closer to being the go-to Sunday morning family lunch, or Saturday date-night experience. We hope that koi lets you take a step back, and transport yourself to faraway lands.

_koi delivers across Bombay & Bangalore, but sends its love across the world. Find us at cookwithkoi.com._

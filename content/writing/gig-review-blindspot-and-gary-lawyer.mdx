_A review of Blindspot and Gary Lawyer's gig at the Hard Rock Cafe, Worli. Original article can be found [here.](http://www.chordsunited.com/blog/reviews/2/45/1/gig-review-blindspot-gary-lawyer.html)_

Somewhere between an indifferent, oblivious events show and a rather intense burger eating competition, the Hard Rock Café held its weekly live show. The double bill featured Blindspot, a nascent act and the ubiquitous Gary Lawyer.

As the burger eating concluded (with this author coming a respectable third), on came Blindspot. Wrongly dubbed as the ‘Vikas Tandon Band’ by the compere, the band was quick to correctly identify themselves as Blindspot. Vocalist Harsh Singh announced, much to the delight of the audience, that the band would be playing a set of U2 covers.



Rock veterans, superheroes and mad scientists, there are few positive labels in the music world that would not fit U2. The foursome, whose career has spanned almost half a century and thirteen full-length releases, find it routine to fill the world’s largest arenas, day in and day out. They have managed to maintain and cement their position as one of the world’s greatest live acts, and have wavered very little (see: Pop, Zooropa) from the sound they introduced the world to. Living up to the hype and expectations that come with ‘a U2 tribute’ is no easy task, but is one that Blindspot were prepared to take on.

Four fortysomethings and their drummer Avartan Bokil stormed through their set with sheer joyousness and contentment on their faces. The band was cautiously maneuvered U2 hits that we all love, showing U2 the correct amount of respect – anymore and they would border on boring, any less and they would have lost what makes the band great. The vocalist was in his entire Bono garb, barring the shades, emulating his gestures and endearing stage presence to the T. He even had the tambourine. Often shrouded into obscurity by the fame of Bono and the Edge, Adam Clayton and his Indian protégé Amit Ahuja provide the band with an incredible, much-needed stability. Turned out in the typical ’I don’t care’ bassist look, Amit was in a perpetual jig through the set. His bass tone, however, was stoic and enduring, holding the band together. Vocalist and guitarist Vikas brought with him layers in both the vocal and instrumental fora, something that often goes under the scanner in U2 songs. With only one live guitarist, it is not easy for the band to fill the entire sonic spectrum, something which both the guitarists were able to do in tandem. The section of the set which began with an acoustic rendition of the evergreen ‘I Still Haven’t Found What I’m Looking For’ which flowed into ‘With Or Without You’ stood out not for its resonance with the audience but because of the incredible restraint displayed by the band. Lead guitarist Hanez spectacularly played the entire e-bow section of the intro to ‘With Or Without You’, and was clearly the standout performer. The hardest part of covering any U2 song is crafting the Edge’s guitar sound, be it the synth-esque sound on ‘Beautiful Day’ or the heavily delayed one on ‘City of Blinding Lights’, but it was something Hanez was able to do with unbelievable candour and effortlessness. To close their power-packed set, the band chose ‘City of Blinding Lights’, a song I have grown to love as a U2 fan. From the get go itself, the drums began a slightly premature build-up, which culminated in an empty transition. The rhythm guitar was inaudible, and the presence of the piano in the introduction was evidently lacking. However, as the performance moved on it garnered steam, with the immaculate guitar tone and vocals stealing the show. The pinnacle of the entire performance was the last line of the second verse, which makes the use of an incredibly subtle transition in guitar tone which was executed flawlessly. ‘Where the Street Have No Name’ was amalgamated with its predecessor, with everything from the delayed intro to the scratchy verses matching the U2 standard.

The set was an enjoyable, well-constructed and entertaining one, with Warren Mendonsa’s doppelganger in both skill and looks, Hanez elevating the performance to the next level.

Up next was a man who has seen the independent music scene being born, decades ago, and has witnessed growth into the blossoming industry it is today. Gary Lawyer, described by many as a living legend and my mother as ‘Our Elvis’, brought to the café a wonderful reminder of the joys of yesteryear. His music was well-rehearsed and masterfully performed, with his grasp over the crowd testament to his legend status. He is a ‘rock star’ in the truest since of the word, with his on-stage antics being met with rapturous applause and acclaim. His band powered through every frill, piano fill, count-in and outro with a sense of ease and jubilance. The band moved in synchronized, organized ways with Gary receiving requests after every song on tissue paper (could have been girls’ phone numbers). Through the blues, country and rock n roll parts of the set, the band was well aware of every aspect of the performance. Their knowledge and mutual respect ensured that Gary had the platform and freedom he deserved to put on the show of his life.

The onstage energy that Gary and his band provided was met with a similar fervor from the alcohol-infused crowd, fusing all forms of dance – tango, break dancing and ballet – into inebriated and entertaining sequence of events.

On the whole, the night can be deemed a success, with the patrons being given a double bill of songs they were all familiar with. The most worrying aspect of the night was the lack of original music – for one of the only music venues in the city to give their only night of live music to two cover acts does not bode well for the future, where independent artists and bands should be encouraged.

### Die on a hill

Used in conversation about the journalistic ethos of After EOD, die on a hill has quickly become a mindset for me to develop. To commit to something, wear it on my sleeve and see it through is something I haven't had to do in such a naked way before. I've always had the scaffolding of a company (not my money or my business, so my commitment was convenient) -- like Scaler or Zilingo -- or the privilege to distance myself (as with koi, where I posted a sum-total of 1 Instagram story about it).

Until now, the process of this violent commitment was unnecessary. Now it is. To begin to unhide and genuinely obsess over things I am building. It is a fascinating exercise to engineer obsession (or love). Previous iterations of this obsession with things I was working on are tempting and easy to compare to -- sacrificing all facets of my life to make things happen, fuelled only by adrenaline.

As I learnt through a book club earlier today, this obsession is similar to younger romantic love -- hormonal, destructive and so addicting. The 2024 view on cultivating obsession is also like dating in 2024, sober and intentional, and ultimately boring. To find the impossible balance between these two is a tall order, yet the process of doing so is thrilling.

As a wise man said this month -

Don't just trust the process, respect it, blemishes & all
Win it, lose it, win it back & then some, Kipling was right

### Be above it

Named after the Tame Impala song, Lonerism is criminally underrated and a relic of the golden era of music (this is not up for debate)

Alternative title: the perils of living too close to home

Moving back to Bombay is a decision -- by and large -- that has greatly improved my mental and physical health. Yet, the feeling of being perceived is significantly higher in Bombay than in Bangalore. Nothing is just your business - it is also everyone around yours. It is consuming to regularly be concerned about perception.

This is not specifically about the mini-golf business, it is generally about living at home in Bombay. The nagging yet necessary questions of "oh who are you going out with" and the inevitable meeting of people you know whenever you go out and the smallness of the world make the instances of being perceived higher than in Bangalore, but perhaps the intensity of this perception is slightly lower.

I also am aware that these are, in fact, very good things and all part of the reasons why I moved back. The takeaway here is to hone space for myself, a solitude I thoroughly enjoyed living away from home. To rebuild intention into my time allocation, and to eventually come to terms with this perception. I am hoping that this mindfulness will bear fruit, allowing me to process this feeling of being perceived rather than reacting to it, or fearing it, as I do currently.

### Put tru men

Put tru men is Bandra-speak for asking your teammate on the football pitch to "put a through ball, man". A through ball, as defined by ChatGPT, is a type of pass that is played between or behind the opposing defenders, aiming to reach a teammate who is making a forward run.

This section is called put tru men for two reasons:

The first reason to call this "put tru men" is that I long for a through ball. To have someone anticipate my run and be able to skillfully locate me (wow this sounds like i want a gf). I like to think that through balls are the clearest representations of team chemistry, and therefore performance (in both football and the office). The trust that your teammate is making that run, that passing to them is a net-positive for your team, and in your own abilities to make the pass. As my weeks as being a single founder compound, I've abruptly realised that I have taken for granted the feeling of playing and receiving through balls. With koi and Scaler, through balls were played left, right and centre, or more literally, upward, downward and sideways. Those I worked with - whether equals/"peers" as Aaron was with koi, downwards (as a "manager") or upward with my managers, the feeling of being forced to make the run and push yourself is one that I miss. It is filled generally by those in my life, but to have it with deep, shared context and objectives (as it was), is unparalleled.

### What is this and why am I doing this?

Hello. This is a monthly newsletter update thingi that I’d like to do to stay more connected with my friends. It is also my attempt at vulnerability, which I’m working on. If you are reading this, you are important to me and our friendship is something I value. Thank you for taking the time out.

This will also not be good writing, but it will be simple and unnuanced.

Also - feel free to reply/not reply at your own pace/convenience. The goal with this is not to force conversation or engage. It is to asynchronously, honestly and (within reasonable bounds) meaningfully remain “in touch” or connected with people I care about.

### Reflections a year on from starting this

This project started about exactly a year ago. In most ways, the primary objective of "staying in touch" has been fulfilled. It is unfortunate but true that feeling disconnected, alienated and lonely has only been compounded by the internet these days.

This was a reaction to this culture of loneliness, in some way to take back some control of friendships, to be able to nurture and preserve them in a more active, intentional manner, somewhat jaded by reel sending and story replying that I became accustomed to.

A year on, therefore, I think I can say with some conviction that this has helped me remain more deeply integrated in all of your lives than I probably would have been if this did not exist. While not applicable to everyone who reads this, at an aggregate level, it is true. Writing and sending this out has helped stay in touch.

So what else could this be. Could this be a way to ease newer friendships? Could this also help me be a better friend to people already in my life? Could this became some pseudo-movement of more people writing something like this, so that we can all remain in each other's lives? I am hopeful that all of these will happen this year, albeit with some tweaks and some awkwardness, which I am ok with.

### Parallel quest

I believe that the act of love is some sort of revolutionary act at a time where institutions are designed to alienate and separate people. And l do also believe that my friendships are generally filled with love of varying qualities.

What happens then when these friendships begin to fray -- when jokes aren't funny anymore, when conversation is forced and when we grapple with seemingly insurmountable context asymmetry? This is inevitable. Explain to an older friend or sibling and they will guarantee that the fragmentation of friendships is par for the course in your mid (to late) 20s. That growing apart is one of those inescapable truths that every generation tries to fight until you wake up one day and accept a decayed friendship for what it is, a point of very painful return.

After this decay is identified and accepted, it is easy to be avoidant. Taking the somewhat easy way out of replacing friends and situations with new ones that you now enjoy more. This is logical and a healthy coping mechanism -- shared context and therefore generally more relevant conversation is a huge part of any deep friendship. Yet it is premised on some sort of natural selection type logic, where only certain people remain relevant as you navigate phases of your life.

I do think that a divergence of context is inevitable, but I think with some effort, the fallout of this divergence can be minimised. Divergent context (caused by different stages of life, different locations and different worldviews) can be reframed as "parallel questing". Previously what was a "battle royale" sort of life, where you and your friends were on the same team fighting the rest of the world, completing the same quests, hand in hand, is now an asynchronous experience. The game still remains the same, but the quests and the teams are very different. Yet, the game remains the same and hence "parallel questing", where each person is tackling similar quests but in a different order, using a different strategy and on a different map.

I've found it easy to forget that the game still remains the same -- that even if someone lives half the world away, studying something I can only feign understanding in, there is much that we have in common. This has been a powerful realisation, as it has helped me try and minimise the impact of divergent context and try and find new common ground or common context, that then allows the unlocking of a new level in a friendship.

This is somewhat easier to do in smaller groups, where there is enough space and time for these contexts to emerge and unionise, but it is significantly harder in larger groups because the number of dynamics scales exponentially as group sizes increase. Yet, foregoting that this is "just how things are" and going on with life well aware that things have changed seems avoidant and lazy. Making a concerted effort to create a new shared context (through an activity, a game or an event) helps create a fresh twist on an old classic, or to induce a space where personal context can be revealed secularly, across a group (like asking questions that everyone in a group can share some perspective on) seem viable. Preserving a group and the dynamic it carries is (its soul) is a perennial, universal issue, but I believe a large part of it comes down to shared purpose and clarity (as is the case in football, and at any well-run organisation), which is sometimes uncomfortable to do with friends. To make the purpose of the group or more specifically the group hang explicit is taboo, and I think more so for largely male friendship groups. To achieve consensus and buy-in on this shared purpose is even harder, yet necessary to preserve the essence of the team. But rejecting the comfort of nostalgia (that is losing potency), or the laziness of the future ("Oh you're here till the 20th? We'll do something on xyz date") and making the most of the anyway fleeting time we have together seems like the move. Discomfort and awkwardness and all.

### Hocus focus

For a long time, my view on New Years was summarised by [this Gramsci essay](https://viewpointmag.com/2015/01/01/i-hate-new-years-day/) —

"That’s why I hate New Year’s. I want every morning to be a new year’s for me. Every day I want to reckon with myself, and every day I want to renew myself. No day set aside for rest. I choose my pauses myself, when I feel drunk with the intensity of life and I want to plunge into animality to draw from it new vigour."

It explains New Years as hegemony, a product promising the reclaiming of identity and agency over oneself. While this is all still true, I now do think using New Years for the logistics of a fresh start is actually kind of helpful.

My self-appointed theme for this year is focus. To cultivate and imbibe focus through discipline and some clarity is essential for Putt One to take shape. Much of 2024 was devoted to building systems and creating a base to grow off of. That has happened. It is now time to start playing :)

This focus has harsh opposition in the fear of missing out, pulling me in different directions and disrupting discipline from being created. It shall be seen how these can be balanced, as I suspect striking this balance is crucial to see sustained, meaningful results in all parts of my life.

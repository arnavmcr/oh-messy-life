### Turn-based living

September affirmed a long-standing but masked habit of mine: in times of high-stress or flux, I cope using video games. In 2021 after koi ended, it was Age of Empires, followed by Civilization 6 and FIFA Ultimate Team in 2023/24, and now my latest coping game is competitive Pokemon, a 'turn based strategy' game.

I've thoroughly enjoyed TBS (turn based strategy) games, even more than RTS (real-time strategy) games. In TBS games, each player makes decisions at the same time but with asymmetric information. So it forces you to run various scenarios, constantly predicting your opponents moves basis the information you have presented.

Since I played Age of Empires back in 2021/22, the idea of finding a "meta" (the most optimal set of decisions you can take to win a game) became highly addicting. Identifying the "meta" wherever possible, at the workplace, while driving, or while actually playing the game, is how I view things -- finding the path of least resistance but highest outcome.

Within each "meta", there is a "macro" and a "micro" component. The macro refers to you setting up an environment that is most conducive to winning, while the micro is how you execute within this environment. I have always thought of myself as a "macro" player and being more adept at creating environments rather than executing within them, and while that may still be true, unlike fucking Pokemon, life/business actually requires you to get both the macro and the micro right.

The real-time decision making and speed that an RTS (say Age of Empires or even FIFA) requires may serve me well as I figure out how to balance thinking and doing.

### Sense and nonsensibilities

For most people I encounter, my default is to try and make sense of them. Through this characterisation, the objective is to be able to empathise or relate better through sense-making without judgement. This is not to say that I believe each person falls into a specific box and they're restricted to a specific trope that has some moral or social hierarchy, but that we're all products of differing ratios of traits.

This addiction to pattern recognition, especially in people, is second nature now, and was honed through the countless interviews I took while at Scaler. I'm unsure of how I feel about this default, but sharing some ways in which I try to make sense of people in my own non-scientific methodology --
